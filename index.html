About MRI Scans

What is an MRI scan?

An MRI scan (Magnetic Resonance Imaging) is a safe and non-invasive medical test that provides detailed images of the inside of your body. It uses strong magnets and radio waves to create these images, and importantly, it does not use radiation, making it a safe option for pregnant women when recommended by your doctor.

Why It's Done During Pregnancy

  • MRI scan are usually recommended only when absolutely necessary, like to investigate certain conditions in the mother (e.g., issues with the brain, spine, or pelvis) or to get detailed images of the baby if an ultrasound isn't sufficient.
  • Your doctor will explain why it’s needed and how it helps in diagnosing or monitoring any concerns.

Safety and Baby's Well-being

  • MRI scan do not use ionizing radiation (like X-rays or CT scans), so there’s no radiation exposure to the baby.
  • MRIs are generally considered safe in pregnancy, particularly in the second and third trimesters. If performed in the first trimester, it’s only in cases where the benefits far outweigh potential risks.

Noisy Environment

The machine can be quite loud, which might be unsettling. The sounds are completely normal and part of how the machine works, but you’ll be provided with ear protection or music to make you more comfortable.

Maternal MRI Guide

Introduction

Maternal MRI scans provide detailed images of your anatomy during pregnancy. These scans ensure your health and identify any conditions affecting your pregnancy.

Preparation

To prepare for your scan:

  • Wear loose, metal-free clothing.
  • Inform your doctor about implants or medical conditions.
  • Avoid eating a heavy meal before the scan.

During the Scan

You’ll lie comfortably on a table that slides into the MRI machine. The scan is painless and takes about 30-60 minutes. Ear protection will be provided for the noise.

Safety Guidelines

  • Maternal MRIs avoid radiation, making them safe during pregnancy.
  • MRIs are typically avoided during the first trimester unless necessary.
  • Let your doctor know about implants, pacemakers, or allergies.

Post-Scan

After your scan, you can return to normal activities. Your results will be reviewed and shared with your doctor.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are translation services available for non-English speakers?

Yes, NHS hospitals offer translation and interpretation services. Please inform your hospital in advance to arrange these services.

What if I feel anxious about the scan?

It’s normal to feel nervous. Most hospitals allow a companion for support during the scan. You can also request earplugs or music to ease discomfort.

Can I bring my children to the appointment?

It’s best to arrange childcare, as children are generally not allowed in the scanning room for safety reasons.

Do I need to pay for the MRI scan as a visitor?

Visitors may need to pay unless they qualify for free NHS treatment under specific agreements or conditions. Check with your hospital or refer to NHS Overseas Visitor Guidance.

Fetal MRI Guide

Introduction

Fetal MRI scans provide detailed images of your baby to check for developmental concerns or issues that may not be visible on an ultrasound.

Preparation

To prepare for your scan:

  • Wear metal-free, comfortable clothing.
  • Provide your doctor with a detailed medical history.
  • Follow any specific instructions from your healthcare provider.

During the Scan

During the scan, you’ll lie on your back or side. The process is painless and takes about 30-60 minutes. Noise is expected, but ear protection is provided.

Safety Guidelines

  • Fetal MRIs avoid ionizing radiation, ensuring safety for you and your baby.
  • They’re generally performed after the first trimester.
  • Inform your doctor if you have implants, pacemakers, or allergies.

Post-Scan

You can resume normal activities immediately after the scan. Results will be reviewed and shared with your doctor.
